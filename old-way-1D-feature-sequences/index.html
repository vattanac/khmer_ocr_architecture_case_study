The Old Way — 1D Feature Pipeline

How traditional OCR tried (and struggled) to read Khmer text

🎯 FIRST — What Does "Feature Sequence" Even Mean?

A computer cannot "read" an image directly. The OCR model first needs to translate the image into numbers. Those numbers are called features. When they are arranged in a list (one after another), it becomes a feature sequence.

💡 Simple Analogy

Think of the image as a long sentence written on a strip of paper. A feature sequence is what you get when you describe each small section of that strip in numbers. Instead of "ក looks like this", you get a vector of numbers describing that patch — produced by the CNN in Step 2.

⚠️ IMPORTANT — Where do those numbers [0.8, 0.1, 0.4, …] actually come from?
▼ explain

Those specific values are illustrative placeholders only — they were chosen to show the shape of a vector, not real data. In reality, the numbers are produced by the CNN (Convolutional Neural Network) in Step 2. Here is the real process:

1
The raw image pixels at position 5 are a small rectangular patch of grey values — a 4×4 patch of numbers between 0 (black) and 255 (white), describing the ink strokes of the character at that location.
2
The CNN applies 256 learned filters (convolution kernels) to that patch. Each filter detects one specific visual pattern — horizontal edges, curved strokes, the circular loops common in Khmer consonants, and so on.
3
Each filter produces one activation number. High = pattern strongly present. Low = not present. After ReLU, negatives become 0.

0.8 in dim 1 → "filter 1 (horizontal edge) was strongly detected here"
0.1 in dim 2 → "filter 2's pattern was barely present"
4
All 256 activation numbers are stacked into one vector → that is the feature vector for position 5. The values are determined entirely by what the CNN learned during training — no human sets these numbers manually.
A vector of D = 256 numbers = the outputs of 256 CNN filters at that position. The full sequence of n such vectors is the feature sequence — the image translated into structured numbers the Transformer can reason about.

🔷 What exactly IS one "position"?

The CNN produces a feature map of shape 4 rows × 62 columns × 256 channels. Look at just one column — say column 7. At that column you have 4 rows × 256 channels = 1,024 numbers. In the 1D pipeline, those 4 rows get collapsed into 1 (averaged), leaving just 256 numbers. That bundle is called one position.

TR_ENC — Transformer Encoder · Full Technical Deep-Dive
Self-Attention mechanics · Q/K/V · Score matrix · O(n²) derivation · Khmer bottleneck
The Transformer Encoder receives the sequence P (248 × 256) from PATCH_ENC and transforms it into a context-enriched sequence T (248 × 256) using Self-Attention. Every position learns from every other position before the decoder reads. This section explains exactly how — and why it is expensive.
🧠 Part 1 — Why does each position need to see others?
▲ collapse

After PATCH_ENC, each vector in the sequence describes only its own small patch of the image — it has no awareness of neighbouring patches. But in Khmer, one character's meaning depends heavily on what is around it:

EXAMPLE — The cluster ក្រី (poor/pitiable)
ក
p1 · base
consonant ក

p2 · below
subscript ្រ
ី

vowel ី
ក
p1 alone knows it is a base consonant — but it needs to look at p2 (្រ) to know a subscript modifies it, and at p3 (ី) to know a vowel follows. Without context: p1 might just output "ក". With context: p1 outputs "ក with subscript ្រ and vowel ី".
្រ
p2 (្រ) is a subscript — it is visually small and only meaningful when paired with its host consonant p1 (ក). Without attending to p1, the model cannot tell which consonant it modifies.
ី
p3 (ី) is a dependent vowel — it is invalid alone. It must attend to p1 (ក) to determine its sound and to p2 (្រ) to know the full cluster context.
Conclusion of Part 1: A model that reads each position independently (like early RNNs or 1D CNNs) will often misread Khmer clusters. Self-attention solves this by letting every position broadcast and receive information from every other position simultaneously.
⚙️ Part 2 — How Self-Attention Works: Q, K, V
▲ collapse

Self-attention uses three learned linear projections of each vector — called Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V). These come from the same input vector but serve different roles:

Q — Query
"What am I looking for?"
Each position generates a Query — a vector that represents what information it needs from others.
Q = W_Q · p_i
K — Key
"What do I offer?"
Each position also generates a Key — a vector that represents what it contains, available for others to match against.
K = W_K · p_j
V — Value
"What do I actually send?"
The Value is the actual content that gets passed when attention is high — the information payload.
V = W_V · p_j
Step-by-step computation for one position i attending to position j:
A
Compute raw attention score
score(i, j) = Q_i · K_jT / √d_k
Dot product of Query_i and Key_j measures how well position i's question matches position j's answer. Dividing by √d_k (where d_k is the key dimension, typically 64) keeps dot products in a stable range during training — without it, large values push softmax into its saturation region where gradients become near-zero, making learning grind to a halt.
B
Softmax — convert scores to probabilities
α(i, j) = softmax( score(i, 1..n) )_j
For position i, we compute scores against all n positions at once, then softmax normalises them to sum to 1. The result α(i,j) is the attention weight — how much position i will "listen to" position j.

C
Weighted sum of Values → new vector for position i
output_i = Σj=1..n α(i,j) · V_j
Position i's new vector is a weighted blend of all n Value vectors. High α(i,j) means position j's Value contributes a lot to position i's output. This is how ក "absorbs" information from ្រ and ី — they contribute their Values with high weight.
FULL SELF-ATTENTION FORMULA (from Vaswani et al. 2017)
Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax( QKT / √dk ) · V
Q = n×d_k matrix  ·  K = n×d_k matrix  ·  V = n×d_v matrix  ·  output = n×d_v matrix

This n×n score matrix must be fully computed — there are no shortcuts in vanilla self-attention. For n=248, that is 248×248 = 61,504 scores to compute, store, and operate on.
📈 Part 4 — O(n²): The Quadratic Explosion
▲ collapse

"O(n²)" is Big-O notation. It means: if n grows by a factor of k, the cost grows by k². Here is what that means concretely when you double the image width:

Original image width W  → sequence length n
Attention cost = n × n = n²
Double image width 2W  → sequence length 2n
Attention cost = (2n) × (2n) = 4n²

⚠️ Why Khmer is the worst case
Latin OCR workaround: Crop each word individually (32×100 px) → n ≈ 13 → n² ≈ 169 pairs. Manageable.
Khmer problem: No word spaces — you cannot crop words. A 10-word Khmer sentence might be 1500–4000 px wide → n ≈ 187–500 → n² ≈ 35,000–250,000 pairs. Memory usage for the n×n matrix alone can exceed GPU limits. Training on many such sequences simultaneously (mini-batches) makes it orders of magnitude more expensive than Latin OCR.
🔑 Full technical summary of Step 4:

1. Each position creates Q, K, V vectors via learned projections.
2. The score matrix QKᵀ / √d_k (shape n×n) is computed — this is the expensive step.
3. Softmax converts each row to attention weights α(i, · ) summing to 1.
4. Each position's new vector = weighted sum of all V vectors using those weights.
5. The n×n score matrix means cost is O(n²) in both time and memory.
6. For Khmer (no word boundaries, arbitrarily wide images), n is large and n² explodes — making vanilla self-attention impractical.
7. The paper's solution: chunk the image first, so each chunk has a small fixed n, reducing total cost from O(n²) to O(n_chunks · n_chunk²) ≈ O(n) linear.

TR_DEC — Transformer Decoder · Full Technical Deep-Dive
What the decoder does · Autoregressive loop · Cross-attention · SOS/EOS tokens · 131-class output
The Transformer Decoder takes the encoded image context T (248 × 256) from TR_ENC and produces the output text one character at a time, using everything it has already predicted to decide what comes next.
🗺️ Part 1 — The Big Picture: Encoder vs Decoder roles
▲ collapse
TR_ENC (Step 4) — done
Reads the entire image all at once. Produces a rich contextual summary T (248 vectors) that encodes every visual feature with global context.

Think of it as a librarian who has read the whole book and can answer any question about it.
→
T passes
TR_DEC (Step 5) — now
Does not see the image directly. Only sees T (the encoded summary) and the characters it has already predicted. Its job: predict the next character, one at a time.

Think of it as a scribe who asks the librarian questions and writes down the answer letter by letter.
Key insight: The encoder sees everything at once (parallel). The decoder generates sequentially — it cannot see future characters when predicting the current one. This is called autoregressive generation.
🪙 Part 2 — Special Tokens: SOS, EOS, PAD
▲ collapse

The decoder uses three special tokens that are not Khmer characters but control signals for the generation loop:

SOS
Start Of Sentence
The very first input fed to the decoder. It is the signal that says "start generating now". The decoder sees SOS → predicts the first real character. The context sequence Z always begins with SOS: Z = [SOS, y₁, y₂, …].
EOS
End Of Sentence
When the decoder predicts EOS, the generation loop stops. This is how the model knows the full text has been output — it is not limited to a fixed number of characters. The target sequence Y always ends with EOS: Y = [y₁, y₂, …, yL, EOS].
PAD
Padding
Used to pad shorter sequences to the same length in a mini-batch during training (so all samples have equal length tensors). PAD tokens are masked — the model learns to ignore them.

🔁 Part 3 — What "Autoregressive" Actually Means
▲ collapse

Auto = self. Regressive = depends on previous outputs. Autoregressive means: each prediction uses its own previous predictions as input. This creates a loop that runs until EOS is predicted.

THE GENERATION LOOP — step by step for "តាកែវ":
t=0
Input Z = [SOS]  +  T (image context)  →  predict: ត  ✓ appended to Z
t=1
Input Z = [SOS, ត]  +  T  →  predict: ា  ✓ appended
t=2
Input Z = [SOS, ត, ា]  +  T  →  predict: ក  ✓ appended
t=3
Input Z = [SOS, ត, ា, ក]  +  T  →  predict: ែ  ✓ appended
t=4
Input Z = [SOS, ត, ា, ក, ែ]  +  T  →  predict: វ  ✓ appended
t=5
Input Z = [SOS, ត, ា, ក, ែ, វ]  +  T  →  predict: EOS  ⬛ STOP — generation complete
Why is this called "autoregressive"? At each step t, the model uses its own previous output (y₁…y_{t-1}) as the next input. The output regresses onto itself — hence auto-regressive. The key consequence: errors compound. If step t=2 predicts the wrong character, every subsequent step sees that wrong character in its context and may be misled.
🏗️ Part 4 — Inside the Decoder: Three Sub-Layers
▲ collapse

Each Transformer decoder unit has three sequential sub-layers. Understanding all three explains exactly how T (image context) and Z (predicted-so-far) combine to predict the next character.

1
Masked Self-Attention on Z
The decoder runs self-attention on the context sequence Z = [SOS, y₁, …, y_{t-1}]. Masked means: position t can only attend to positions 0…t-1 — it is forbidden from looking at future characters (which don't exist yet at inference, and must not be cheated during training).

This sub-layer lets the decoder build up an understanding of "what have I said so far?"
output₁ = MaskedSelfAttn(Z, Z, Z)  ·  shape: (t × D)
2
Cross-Attention on T (the image context)
This is the critical bridge between image and text. The decoder uses output₁ as the Query ("what do I need from the image?"), and the encoder output T as both the Key and Value ("what does the image contain?").

Concretely: at step t=2 (predicting ក), the decoder's Query asks "given I've already said ត and ា, what part of the image should I look at next?" — and the cross-attention mechanism points to the relevant image region.
output₂ = CrossAttn(Q=output₁, K=T, V=T)  ·  shape: (t × D)
3
Feed-Forward → Linear → Softmax → 131 probabilities
output₂ passes through a Feed-Forward Network (FFN), then a final linear projection maps it from D=256 dimensions to C=131 dimensions — one score per possible output character. Softmax converts these raw scores into a probability distribution over all 131 classes. The 131 classes cover: 33 base consonants, 32 subscripts, ~35 vowels (16 dependent simple + 5 compound + 14 independent), 8 diacritics, 10 numerals, 10 punctuation, and 3 special tokens (SOS/EOS/PAD). The character with the highest probability is selected as ŷ_t.
ŷ_t = argmax( softmax( Linear( FFN(output₂) ) ) )  ·  over C=131 classes

📉 Part 6 — How the Decoder Learns: Cross-Entropy Loss
▲ collapse

During training, the ground-truth sequence Y = [y₁, y₂, …, yL, EOS] is known. At each step t, the decoder predicts a probability distribution Ŷ_t over 131 classes. The loss penalises the model for assigning low probability to the correct character y_t:

CROSS-ENTROPY LOSS (from the paper, equation 5)
Loss = CE(Ŷ, Y) = −Σt log Ŷ_t[y_t]
Ŷ_t = predicted probability distribution at step t  ·  y_t = correct character at step t
If the model predicted 68% for the correct character: loss contribution = −log(0.68) ≈ 0.39 (small, good).
If the model predicted 2% for the correct character: loss contribution = −log(0.02) ≈ 3.91 (large, bad).
Backpropagation adjusts all weights (W_Q, W_K, W_V in both encoder and decoder) to minimise this loss over millions of training examples.
🔑 Full technical summary of Step 5:

1. The decoder receives T (248×256 encoded image context) from TR_ENC and a growing context sequence Z.
2. Generation starts with Z = [SOS] and loops until EOS is predicted.
3. Each decoder step runs three sub-layers: Masked Self-Attention (on Z) → Cross-Attention (Q from Z, K/V from T) → FFN + Linear + Softmax.
4. Cross-attention is the bridge: the decoder queries the image context T to decide what to output next.
5. Softmax produces a probability over C = 131 classes; argmax selects the character ŷ_t.
6. ŷ_t is appended to Z and the loop repeats — this is autoregressive generation.
7. Training uses Cross-Entropy Loss comparing predicted ŷ against ground-truth y at every step.

The maths of O(n²)
If W = 100 px needs 169 attention pairs, then W = 2000 px (20× wider) needs 62,500 pairs — that is 370× more computation, not 20×. This is what O(n²) means: cost grows with the square of length, not linearly.
🔑 What Exactly is D ?

The letter D appears in the paper as the dimension of each feature vector in the sequence. Here is what that means:

TermMeaningExample value
n Number of vectors in the sequence (sequence length). Grows with image width. This is the problem. 248 vectors
D Number of numbers inside each vector (vector dimension). Fixed by model design — does not change with image width. D = 256
Feature sequence The full list: n vectors × D numbers each. Written as a matrix of shape n × D. 248 × 256
Self-attention cost Grows as n² — squaring the sequence length. D does not affect this cost directly. n² = 61,504
So when the paper says "D feature sequences" — it means: a sequence of feature vectors, where each vector has D = 256 dimensions. The problem in the old way is not D (that is fixed and small), but n (which grows with image width and makes self-attention expensive).

✅ Summary in Plain Language
In the old way, the OCR model takes the full-width Khmer image and runs it through a CNN to get a feature map. That map is then flattened into a sequence of n vectors, each vector having D = 256 numbers (the "D feature sequence"). The Transformer Encoder then runs self-attention on those n vectors — but self-attention compares every vector to every other, so it costs n² in memory and time. Since Khmer text lines are wide (no spaces between words), n is large, and n² is huge. This is the core problem the chunking approach (the new way, Figure 15) solves — by splitting the image into short chunks first, so n stays small and cost stays linear O(n) instead of quadratic.
